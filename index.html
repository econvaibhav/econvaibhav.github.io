I'm a first-year doctoral researcher at the Technical University of Munich studying how algorithmic governance and digitalisation shape public trust. I focus on the heuristics people use when making decisions, leveraging computational methods, conjoint experiments, and multimodal approaches (text, audio, image, and video) to explore complex human behaviors and public policy.

I grew up in Bangalore, a city in Southern India, where I pursued my Bachelor of Arts in Economics at Azim Premji University. Before moving to Munich, I traveled to Helsinki to study Social Data Science, graduating with a Master's in Social Sciences from the University of Helsinki. I enjoy presenting my research in accessible and creative formats. Outside of academia, you'll find me exploring my long-standing interests in reading, travel, sports, music, and cooking.

Research

Working papers and publications will appear here as my PhD progresses.

I am in the early stages of my doctoral research at the Professorship for Algorithmic Governance and Public Policy (AGAPP) at the Technical University of Munich. My work sits at the intersection of algorithmic governance, public trust, and computational methods. I explore how algorithmic decision-making (ADM) systems are perceived by the public, trying to understand what drives legitimacy and trust. Methodologically, I focus on conjoint experiments and explore how methods like latent class analysis can investigate attribute non-attendance. I also heavily utilize multimodal analysis—spanning text, audio, image, and video—to capture nuanced human interactions.
